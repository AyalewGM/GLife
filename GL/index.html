Game of Life

Maharishi University of Management

Conway’s Game of Life gives an example of self-interacting dynamics giving rise to complex structure. Start with an initial pattern and then keep applying four rules of self-interaction—and see what happens!

The universe of the Game of Life is an infinite two-dimension grid of square cells. Each cell is either alive or dead. At each step of life, every cell interacts with its eight neighbors, horizontally, vertically, or diagonally.

The interactions follow the following rules:

  1. Any live cell with one or zero live neighbors dies, caused by under-population.
  2. Any live cell with two or three live neighbors lives on to the next generation.
  3. Any live cell with more than three live neighbors dies by over-population.
  4. Any dead cell with exactly three live neighbors becomes a live cell by reproduction.

The initial pattern constitutes the seed of the system. Apply these rules simultaneously to every cell in the seed to get the first generation. Do this again to get the second generation. Repeat to create each further generation.

Start with a simple initial pattern and see what happens. Then create more complex patterns and see what you get.
